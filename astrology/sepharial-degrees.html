Sepharial Degrees

360 symbolic interpretations, one for each degree of the zodiac, drawn from Kabbalistic and classical astrological principles by British astrologer Sepharial in 1898. A structured, analytical approach to degree symbolism.

| What Are Sepharial Degrees?

Sepharial Degrees are a set of 360 symbolic interpretations, one for each degree of the zodiac, created by the British astrologer and theosophist known as Sepharial. Each degree is assigned a symbolic image, a keyword or phrase, and a descriptive passage that outlines the qualities, tendencies, and potential associated with that specific degree.

Published in 1898 in the same volume as Charubel's degree symbols, The Degrees of the Zodiac Symbolised, Sepharial's system takes a distinctly different approach. Where Charubel relied on clairvoyant vision, Sepharial drew on Kabbalistic, numerological, and classical astrological principles to construct his interpretations. The result is a more analytical and structured set of degree meanings.

His descriptions often include a summary keyword that captures the essential quality of each degree, making them practical reference points for chart interpretation. This structured format gives practitioners a clear, accessible entry point into degree-level analysis.

Sepharial Degrees are symbolic rather than literal. They work best as reflective prompts interpreted through chart context. Each keyword and image is designed to evoke meaning, not to predict specific events.

| Who Was Sepharial?

Sepharial was the pen name of Walter Gorn Old (1864–1929), a British astrologer, theosophist, and numerologist who became one of the most prolific astrological writers of the late 19th and early 20th centuries. He joined the Theosophical Society as a young man and was a contemporary of Madame Blavatsky, living in her London household for a period during the late 1880s.

His approach to astrology was deeply influenced by his study of the Kabbalah, numerology, and Hermetic philosophy. Unlike many astrologers who relied purely on intuition or observation, Sepharial sought to ground his work in systematic, esoteric frameworks. This intellectual rigor is reflected in his degree symbols, which tend to be more structured and descriptive than those produced through purely visionary methods.

Over the course of his career, Sepharial published dozens of books and pamphlets on astrology, numerology, and esoteric subjects. His degree symbols, published alongside Charubel's in 1898, represent one of his most enduring contributions to the field. They continue to be used by practitioners who value their analytical clarity and their grounding in classical esoteric tradition.

Sepharial's blending of Kabbalistic principles with classical astrology produced a degree symbol system that is distinct in character from any other. His analytical approach complements the more intuitive systems that came before and after.

| Sepharial vs Other Degree Systems

Sepharial Degrees sit alongside Charubel Degrees and Sabian Symbols as one of three major degree symbol systems available in astrology. Each was created independently and carries its own distinct qualities. Here is how they compare:

Origin

Sepharial's symbols were published in 1898, the same year as Charubel's, in the same volume. The Sabian Symbols followed nearly 30 years later in 1925. All three systems were created independently, each reflecting the creator's unique perspective and method.

Method

Sepharial drew on Kabbalistic, numerological, and classical astrological frameworks to construct his interpretations. Charubel relied on solitary clairvoyant vision. The Sabian process involved a structured collaboration between a clairvoyant and an astrologer. Each method produced a different quality of symbolism.

Tone and Style

Sepharial's descriptions tend to be more analytical and structured, often including a keyword or phrase that captures the essential quality of each degree. Charubel's are more concise and mystical. Sabian Symbols are generally more narrative and scenic, describing human situations and landscapes.

Using Multiple Systems

Comparing all three systems for the same degree can be especially powerful. Where the symbols agree, the theme may be particularly strong. Where they differ, each system illuminates a different facet of the degree's meaning, giving you a richer and more nuanced interpretation.

No single degree system is more "correct" than the others. They are independent symbolic lenses, each valuable on its own and especially useful when compared side by side.
